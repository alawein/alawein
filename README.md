## About

<!-- SYNC:PROFILE:START -->
<table>
<tr>
<td width="55%" valign="top">

Computational scientist, AI systems engineer, and founder—working at the intersection of scientific computing, HPC, LLM systems, and AI infrastructure. I hold a PhD in EECS from UC Berkeley, where my work centered on materials simulation, multiscale modeling, and workflow automation for computational science. Over time, that foundation expanded from DFT-driven HPC systems and ML-accelerated materials discovery into LLM alignment, evaluation, and infrastructure for reliable AI-assisted engineering. I've built automated DFT workflows, high-throughput screening systems, and evaluation pipelines for research-grade reasoning. The throughline never changed, only the surface area: if outputs cannot be verified, what are we actually scaling?

My work lives at a rare intersection between research and production—where the rigor of computational physics meets the scale of modern AI, and where elegant theory is expected to survive real-world failure modes. At Lawrence Berkeley National Lab, I designed and deployed high-throughput simulation workflows that transformed raw compute into actionable materials insight, optimizing resource allocation across HPC environments at a scale large enough to recalibrate your intuitions about efficiency. That experience left a permanent imprint: efficiency and reliability aren't aspirational qualities, they're engineered constraints.

That foundation didn't stay contained. What began with physics-driven HPC systems and ML-accelerated materials discovery expanded naturally into LLM alignment, evaluation frameworks, and the less visible—but far more consequential—infrastructure required for trustworthy AI systems. The trajectory was almost inevitable: the same principles that make a DFT pipeline robust—reproducibility, fault tolerance, structured outputs, and tight validation loops—are exactly what LLM systems lack by default. It's not surprising that these systems fail. What's interesting is that they fail in ways we already know how to address. Today, I build and advise on AI infrastructure where correctness matters: scientific pipelines, engineering copilots, and evaluation systems that don't just demonstrate capability, but systematically interrogate it—probing, stress-testing, and validating behavior under pressure. I founded Morphism Systems around a belief that is intentionally unsophisticated: the most valuable AI systems are the ones you can trust; not as retrofitted safeguards, but as foundational design principles. That means treating alignment, evaluation, and infrastructure as first-class citizens from day one. My work, spanning APS, IEEE, and ACS publications, now focuses on making complex AI systems dependable, enforceable, and grounded in real-world engineering workflows—systems that don't just run, but endure.

## Governance

This org runs on a single operating contract: [workspace-master-prompt](docs/governance/workspace-master-prompt.md). Core tenets:

- **Single source of truth** — `SSOT.md`, per-repo `AGENTS.md`, org `README.md`
- **Scope before action** — boundaries defined; violations rejected with file/line evidence
- **Observable changes** — commit style `type(scope): description`
- **README sync** — structural changes flow through `sync-readme.py` and the doc contract
- **Design & branding** — shared tokens in [devkit/tokens](https://github.com/alawein/devkit/tree/main/tokens); brand manifests in [repz/branding](https://github.com/alawein/repz/tree/main/branding). Canonical paths: [workspace-resource-map](docs/governance/workspace-resource-map.md).

**Quality gates:** `python scripts/sync-readme.py --check` · `./scripts/validate-doc-contract.sh --full`
